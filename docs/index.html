Product brief

Araponga is a service for communities that want local visibility without giving up autonomy. Everything starts with territory (território): a real geographic cut that organizes the experience, defines context, and reduces noise. From it, two essential human layers emerge: visitor and resident. These roles are not just “authorization details”; they are the mechanism that sustains digital coexistence with respect for território and for people.

Instead of a global feed that captures attention, Araponga establishes a feed and a map that respond to território and to the bond. This allows the platform to prioritize what matters, protect sensitive content, and cultivate territorial memory — including, in the future, media anchored to coordinates that can compose richer layers and even spatial experiences (including 3D) when it makes sense.

Technical focus: domain-driven architecture, simple and auditable contracts, layered governance, and incremental evolution (MVP → advanced layers → spatial experiences anchored in territory). The goal is to build a solid core before accelerating features.

Domains

The domains exist to keep responsibilities clear and prevent social rules from mixing with representations of território. The intent is to protect system integrity: territory as reference, people as bond, content as expression, and governance as an explicit layer. Below are the essentials in the current stage.

For technical reasons, the entities in the codebase are named in English (Territory, User, Membership, Feed, Map, Media, GeoAnchor), even when we discuss território in Portuguese.

Territory

What it is: a neutral geographic cut — the organizing reference of Araponga.

Why it exists: separates “where” (território) from “who” (people and bonds) and from “what” (content and layers). This lets the system evolve without distorting the core concept.

  • Fields: id, name, slug, bounds, createdAt.
  • Endpoints: GET /api/v1/territories, GET /api/v1/territories/{id}.

User

What it is: authenticated identity to act in the system.

Why it exists: makes actions accountable without exposing sensitive data. The rule is: collect the minimum necessary, keep traceability of what matters, and protect what does not need to exist.

  • Fields: id, displayName, email, status.
  • Endpoints: POST /api/v1/auth/login, POST /api/v1/auth/register.

Membership

What it is: the bond between a person and a territory (visitor or resident).

Why it exists: governs visibility, access, and local priorities. This domain is the operational heart of the rules: without it, the territory becomes a generic filter; with it, território becomes a context for coexistence.

  • Fields: id, userId, territoryId, role, status.
  • Endpoints: GET /api/v1/territories/{territoryId}/membership.

Feed

What it is: posts, alerts, and events tied to territory.

Why it exists: distributes relevant information with visibility rules. The feed is “what is alive” in the territory: reports, notices, invitations, and memory in time.

  • Fields: id, title, content, type, visibility, createdAtUtc.
  • Endpoints: GET /api/v1/feed, POST /api/v1/feed.

Map

What it is: georeferenced entities and layers of territory.

Why it exists: turns information into spatial reference. The map is not “just another screen”: it is the mechanism that situates what matters without depending on global noise and without standardizing território as a product.

  • Fields: id, name, category, status, visibility, createdAtUtc.
  • Endpoints: GET /api/v1/map/entities, POST /api/v1/map/entities.

Media + GeoAnchor

What it is: media (photo/audio/video) anchored in latitude/longitude (and, when useful, altitude).

Why it exists: builds territorial memory with precision — and with control. GeoAnchor lays the groundwork for future layers: contextual visualization, trails, care records, and eventually more complex spatial representations (including 3D) generated from app data and media.

  • Fields: mediaId, territoryId, lat, lon, altitude, visibility.
  • Endpoints: planned (upcoming).

Primary flows

Two flows define the initial experience. Both are guided by territory and sustained by membership. The goal is not just to “work”: it is to work with social and spatial coherence in território.

Flow 1 — Onboarding

  1. Login or account creation.
  2. Choice of territory (or definition via local context, when applicable).
  3. Bond declaration: visitor or resident.
  4. Visibility resolution: feed and map respond to the active role and territory.

Result: the user enters a territory as a real context — not a generic feed. The platform operates with explicit coexistence and access rules.

Flow 2 — Georeferenced post

  1. Verified resident creates a post with media or narrative.
  2. Geographic association to territory (and eventually to a point or trail).
  3. Visibility rules define whether it is public or restricted.
  4. Feed and map render according to the user role and governance.

Result: contextualized content with territorial memory, without global noise.

Quickstart

To run the API locally and access Swagger/healthcheck, follow the README steps. The static portal serves as a domain reference while the API evolves.

  1. Clone the repository and restore dependencies.
  2. Run dotnet run --project backend/Araponga.Api.
  3. Access /swagger and /health on the configured host.

Principles and governance

Araponga follows principles of data care, rule transparency, and local autonomy. Governance evolves in layers: territory as a base, bonds as a filter, curation as safety, and auditing as a memory of decisions.

  • Privacy by design: collect the minimum possible and protect what is sensitive.
  • Local governance: visibility and curation decisions respect território.
  • Incremental evolution: build the core before expanding scope.
  • Non-extractivism: technology must not capture território as a product.
